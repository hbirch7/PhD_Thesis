\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
Over the past century, there has been an abundance of evidence to suggest that the majority of the mass density of the universe is composed of non-luminous/non-interacting matter. Weakly Interacting Massive Particles (WIMPs) are a leading candidate motivated by both theoretical and cosmological arguments. The LUX-ZEPLIN experiment employs a dual-phase liquid xenon Time Projection Chamber (TPC) to detect WIMP-nucleon interactions. The TPC is surrounded by veto system consisting of an instrumented xenon skin layer (Skin) and gadolinium-loaded liquid scintillator (GdLS) Outer Detector (OD), primarily used to veto neutron backgrounds which mimic WIMP interactions in the TPC.

An array of 120 photomultiplier tubes (PMTs) observe scintillation light from the interaction of $\gamma$-rays produced by neutrons which capture in the GdLS. This work presents to commissioning of the Outer Detector and the single photoelectron calibration of the OD PMTs which enables single photoelectron sensitivity required to observe low energy interactions in the OD. Through-tout the WS2024 science run, a single photoelectron sensitivity stability of $(1.37\pm0.25)\%$ at an operating gain of $2\times10^6$ gain has been achieved.

Improvements to the Outer Detector simulation geometry have been made and these improvements are reflected in the comparison between the measured neutron capture timing observed in calibration data sets, with good agreement. The optimisation of the veto coincidence selection for the 2024 WIMP search analysis resulted in an estimated neutron tagging efficiency of $(92\pm4)\%$ with a 3\% impact on detector live time. This result presents a factor of two reduction in live time impact by the veto coincidence selection whilst maintain the same tagging efficiency achieved in the WS2022 analysis. The veto coincidence selection was used to generate a sample data set which is used to estimate the number of single scatter events which result from background detector neutrons. An expectation of $0.0^{0.2}$ single scatter events result from the background detector neutrons in the 2024 WIMP search.

To further understand the neutron background of LZ, an initial muon flux measurement has be performed to understand the rate of atmospheric muons through the detector which subsequently can produce neutrons through interactions with the detector and surrounding material. A reconstructed flux of $(5.119 \pm 0.080 \textrm{( stat.)} \pm 0.049 \textrm{ (syst.)})\times10^{-9}$~cm$^{-2}$~s$^{-1}$ is derived from the ratio of muon rate found in data and simulation taking into account the muon flux used in the simulation. In addition to the muon flux measurement, the best fit diurnal muon modulation frequency $f=(1/21.7)^{+0.005}_{-0.007}~\text{day}^{-1}$ is found with a local significance of $3.05\sigma$ with $p=0.001$. In addition to the muon flux measurement, the muon veto and hold off has been developed to remove events which follow muon tagged events. The veto is intended to remove events which have high rates of ionisation pulses which are produced by muons passing through the TPC. The veto was initially developed for the WS2022 science run and later optimised for the WS2024 science run. The muon veto has an impact of 1.3\% on the WS2024 detector live time. 

Finally, the 2024 WIMP search analysis performed by LZ is presented. The analysis produced the world-leading limits on spin-independent WIMP-nucleon interactions by a factor of four, for WIMP masses $>9~\text{GeV}/c^2$.
